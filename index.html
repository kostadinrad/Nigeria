Nigeria iGaming Strategic Report – March 2026

Following the November 2024 Supreme Court ruling that confined the National Lottery Act to the Federal Capital Territory, Nigeria has moved decisively to a state‑led model coordinated by the Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN).[web:8][web:53][web:51][web:27][web:43] The country now hosts one of Africa’s largest gambling markets, supported by a young, mobile‑first population and recent fiscal reforms that standardise gaming taxes and licence fees across states.[web:55][web:56][web:60][web:62]

Nigeria’s gambling industry is projected to generate about $3.63 billion by end‑2025, with over 60 million Nigerians placing bets and online gambling turnover estimated at ₦5.6 trillion, underscoring both scale and regulatory importance.[web:55][web:56][web:57][web:60][web:62][web:45][web:40]

1. Market Landscape & Leading Brands

Multiple independent reports confirm that sports betting is the dominant form of gambling in Nigeria, accounting for the majority of the sector’s revenue, driven by widespread football fandom and mobile access.[web:55][web:56][web:60][web:62] Industry commentary consistently cites local brands such as Bet9ja, BetKing and SportyBet among the leading operators, although precise market share percentages (e.g. “35%”) are not officially published and should be treated as indicative rather than hard facts.[web:57][web:60]

  • Target demographic: Estimates suggest that over 60 million Nigerians place bets, with the core segment being 18–40‑year‑old males in urban and peri‑urban areas.[web:55][web:56][web:57][web:60][web:62][web:45]
  • Device usage: The surge in online and mobile betting is tied to smartphone adoption and cheap data; reports note that digital and mobile channels now drive most betting transactions.[web:55][web:56][web:40][web:60][web:62]
The Nigerian iGaming market is large and football‑centric, with tens of millions of regular bettors and a strong shift from retail to online and mobile platforms.[web:55][web:56][web:40][web:60][web:62]

2. Regulatory Hierarchy & Licensing Paths

2.1 State Regulators & FSGRN

The Supreme Court decision clarified that lottery and related games fall under the residual powers of the states, effectively stripping the National Lottery Act of effect outside the FCT and affirming state control over gambling within their borders.[web:8][web:53][web:51] In response, state regulators have coordinated through the Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN), which has introduced a unified 11% tax on Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR) and fixed annual licence fees of ₦100 million per category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino), effective 1 January 2026.[web:27][web:41][web:43][web:30]

The FSGRN regime applies equally to online and land‑based operators, eliminating distinctions between retail and remote operations and requiring all licensees to comply with geo‑fencing and geo‑tracking obligations to respect state boundaries.[web:27][web:41][web:43][web:30]

2.2 NLRC Federal Role & Remote Permits

The National Lottery Regulatory Commission (NLRC) retains its role under the 2005 Act within the FCT and continues to issue licences and remote or offshore permits, but these are now legally effective only in the FCT and are not recognised as sufficient authorisation in the states without corresponding state licences.[web:8][web:53]

Legal analysis stresses that operators relying solely on federal permits to target players in states – without corresponding state licences – face significant regulatory risk after the Supreme Court decision and the subsequent FSGRN standardisation.[web:8][web:53][web:27][web:30]

For commercial planning, the FCT should be treated as a separate, NLRC‑regulated territory, while all other states are regulated via their own laws under an FSGRN‑coordinated tax and fee framework.[web:8][web:53][web:27][web:41][web:43][web:30]

3. Financial & Corporate Entry Barriers

Under the FSGRN framework, all licensed operators must pay a fixed annual fee of ₦100 million for each major category they operate in (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino), alongside the 11% GGR tax; this applies regardless of whether the operation is online or land‑based.[web:27][web:41][web:43][web:30]

4. Fiscal Regime & Effective Burden

From January 2026, all licensed operators in participating states will pay 11% of GGR as a “good causes” tax plus ₦100m per‑category licence fees; industry commentary notes that this is expected to coexist with general corporate taxation and any applicable excise or service‑level taxes.[web:27][web:41][web:43][web:30][web:21][web:35]

Commentary also highlights that, while player‑side withholding taxes on winnings are not uniformly codified at the national level, some states and tax authorities have experimented with or discussed withholding on winnings; operators need flexible systems to implement such measures where formally adopted.[web:27][web:41]

Once GGR tax, licence fees, corporate tax and fees for payments and marketing are combined, an effective 18–22%+ burden on GGR is a reasonable planning range for serious nationwide operators.[web:27][web:41][web:30][web:43][web:21][web:35]

5. Compliance, Monitoring & Marketing Constraints

5.1 Real‑Time Monitoring & Geo‑Fencing

FSGRN announcements emphasise that licensed operators must implement geo‑fencing and geo‑tracking to ensure bets are accepted only in jurisdictions where the operator is licensed, and to support accurate allocation of GGR and tax to each state.[web:27][web:41][web:43]

Although specific platform names differ by state, regulators are converging on API‑based monitoring systems that require operators to feed transactional data (stakes, wins, losses) in real time or near‑real time, replacing manual reporting and log‑based supervision.[web:27][web:41][web:44]

5.2 Payments & Advertising Restrictions

The sector’s growth is closely linked to digital payments, with market reporting tying the 3.63 billion USD market projection to rapid adoption of mobile and online payments for gambling.[web:55][web:56][web:40][web:60][web:62] Payment processors and wallets remain central to onboarding and retention, although individual fee levels and integration requirements are commercial rather than regulatory specifics.

A major new constraint is the Google ban on gambling advertising targeting Nigeria, effective 8 January 2025, which prohibits the promotion of gambling products and related materials across Search, YouTube and the Display Network.[web:58][web:59][web:61] This forces operators to pivot to alternative channels such as affiliates, influencers, SMS, in‑app cross‑promotion and offline sponsorships.

Marketing efficiency in Nigeria has been structurally reduced by the Google ad ban, increasing CAC and pushing operators toward more expensive or less scalable acquisition channels.[web:58][web:59][web:61]

7. Strategic Takeaways for Operators

  • Scale is essential: the fixed ₦100m per‑category licence fee and 11% GGR tax mean operators must reach substantial GGR to approach break‑even.
  • Marketing needs re‑engineering due to the Google ban; operators should plan for higher CAC and design diversified acquisition and retention funnels.[web:58][web:59][web:61]
  • Compliance and technical integration (monitoring, geo‑fencing, data feeds) are non‑negotiable and require experienced teams and robust platforms.[web:27][web:41][web:43][web:30]
