Nigeria iGaming Market Report – Sportsbook, Online Casino & Live Casino

Nigeria is a large, rapidly growing but structurally complex iGaming market (sportsbook, online casino and live casino) with a state‑led regulatory framework, a coordinated 11% GGR tax from 2026, and significant licence and compliance costs.

1. Population & Connectivity

Nigeria has a population in excess of 220 million people, with a very young demographic profile and heavy concentration in major urban centres such as Lagos and Abuja.

Industry reports indicate that the online gambling market is growing at around 16% annually, from about 400 million USD in 2023 to an expected 500 million USD in 2025, driven by widespread smartphone usage, mobile broadband, and local fintech rails.

Analysts estimate that roughly 60% of the population is online and that almost 90% of online bets are placed via smartphones, supported by payment providers such as Flutterwave, Paystack, OPay and PalmPay, which make deposits and withdrawals fast and accessible.

Key insight: Infrastructure and payments are sufficient for a fully digital, mobile‑first ecosystem; the binding constraint is disposable income per bettor, not connectivity.

2. Legalisation Status

Gambling in Nigeria has traditionally been regulated at both federal and state levels, with the National Lottery Act 2005 establishing a federal framework for lotteries and creating the National Lottery Regulatory Commission (NLRC), while states such as Lagos enacted their own gaming laws and regulators for sports betting, casinos and lotteries.

On 22 November 2024, the Supreme Court of Nigeria nullified the National Lottery Act 2005 for all states, holding that the National Assembly lacked the constitutional power to regulate lottery activities outside the Federal Capital Territory (FCT); the Act now only applies in the FCT, while lotteries and games of chance fall under state legislative competence elsewhere.

Following this ruling, state regulators have consolidated their roles, and the Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN) has emerged as a coordinating body to harmonise rules and tax regimes across states, particularly for online and remote gaming.

Key insight: Nigeria is now clearly a state‑led market for lotteries and games of chance, with the NLRC’s remit effectively confined to the FCT; operators must engage state regulators and FSGRN rather than relying on a single federal licence.

3. Regulatory Authority

The National Lottery Regulatory Commission (NLRC) continues to regulate lottery and related games of chance within the FCT and still issues licences for national/remote lottery, sports betting and casino under the 2005 Act for that territory, but its authority is no longer enforceable in the 36 states after the Supreme Court decision.

Each state may create its own gaming regulator and legal framework; Lagos, for example, operates the Lagos State Lotteries and Gaming Authority (LSLGA), which licenses sports betting, lotteries, casinos and related activities within Lagos State, while other states have similar bodies under their individual gaming laws.

The Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN), chaired by the CEO of LSLGA, has adopted a harmonised regime that includes a flat 11% tax on Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR) and a standard annual licence fee of ₦100 million per category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino) across participating states, effective 1 January 2026.

Key insight: From 2026, FSGRN rules give Nigeria a de‑facto uniform baseline for gaming tax and licence fees across states, even though regulation remains constitutionally decentralised.

4. Licence Classes & Product Scope

Across NLRC (FCT) and state regulators, the legal framework recognises a range of licence classes that together can support a full iGaming operation (sportsbook, RNG casino, live casino, lotteries and virtuals).

  • Sports betting licences – typically cover pre‑match and in‑play betting on domestic and international sports, plus virtual sports, offered via retail outlets, websites and mobile apps.
  • Casino licences – authorise land‑based casinos and, in many states, online casino products such as slots, table games and live‑dealer offerings, often under the same corporate umbrella.
  • Lottery licences – cover national, state and local draw‑based games, as well as instant‑win products and increasingly mobile/SMS lotteries.
  • Ancillary/other licences – cover gaming machines, route operations, pool betting, promotional games and “other games” that do not fall neatly within standard categories.

Key insight: With the right combination of sports, casino and lottery licences across FCT and key states, a single operator can legally offer sportsbook, RNG casino and live‑dealer products nationwide.

5. Capital, Corporate Structure & Physical Presence

State regulators typically require operators to incorporate a local company, maintain a minimum share capital (often in the tens of millions of Naira), and demonstrate adequate financial capacity; while thresholds vary by state, they function as a hard filter for under‑capitalised applicants.

Operators must maintain a real physical presence in each licensing state, including an administrative office and local staff, and must connect their platforms to regulatory monitoring systems that provide real‑time or near‑real‑time access to betting and payout data.

FSGRN’s standardisation move means that, from 2026, every licensed operator will pay ₦100 million per year for each major category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino), regardless of whether they operate online, retail or both, effectively setting a high fixed‑cost floor for market participation.

Key insight: Nigeria’s capital and presence requirements are designed to filter out small, lightly capitalised operators; serious market entry requires multi‑year financial and operational commitment.

6. Licensing Process & Timelines

Licensing processes typically involve: submission of a detailed application dossier (corporate documents, shareholder and director information, financial statements, business plan), payment of an initial application fee, technical and compliance due diligence, and final licence issuance subject to integration with regulatory monitoring systems.

FSGRN has also indicated that operators will be required to implement geo‑fencing and geo‑tracking to ensure compliance with territorial licensing and to support the application of state‑level taxes and consumer protection rules; these technical requirements add further time to the integration phase.

Market commentary suggests that realistic end‑to‑end timelines from company setup to full operational go‑live can range from 6 to 12 months, depending on the number of states involved and the readiness of the operator’s AML, KYC, and reporting systems.

Key insight: Operators should plan for at least six months of zero‑revenue licensing and technical work before acquiring the first customer at scale.

7. Taxation – Operators

Independent industry and legal analyses agree that Nigeria’s online gambling market is projected to reach around 500 million USD in revenue by 2025, growing at roughly 16% annually, with sports betting as the main revenue driver.

From 1 January 2026, all licensed operators in participating states will face a flat tax rate of 11% on Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR), with funds earmarked for social and community initiatives; this rate applies uniformly across lotteries, sports betting and casinos and is intended to replace a patchwork of earlier state‑level levies.

In addition to the 11% GGR tax, operators must pay an annual licence fee of ₦100 million per category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino), which can be a significant fixed cost, particularly for smaller or mid‑tier companies operating in multiple verticals.

Operators are also subject to general corporate income tax and any applicable withholding or excise taxes linked to telecommunications and gaming services, which can further raise the effective tax burden on GGR, especially for online‑only operators that rely heavily on digital payment channels.

Key insight: A realistic planning assumption for a multi‑state online operator under the 2026 regime is an effective 18–22% burden on GGR once the 11% tax, licence amortisation, and other sectoral levies are factored in.

8. Taxation – Players

FSGRN has highlighted that while the 11% GGR tax and licence fees fall on operators, the question of withholding tax on player winnings remains under the jurisdiction of the individual State Internal Revenue Services, leading to some variation in practice.

Operators have been advised to engage directly with state tax authorities to clarify whether and how withholding tax on payouts should be applied, and to adjust their payout, reporting and reconciliation systems accordingly to avoid future disputes or penalties.

Public debate continues around the fairness and practicality of taxing winnings in addition to taxing operator GGR, particularly given the socio‑economic profile of many Nigerian bettors and the already high tax burden on operators.

Key insight: Player‑side taxation is still in flux; while operators can model scenarios with and without withholding, they must design flexible payout systems to support state‑specific approaches.

9. Compliance, AML/KYC & Responsible Gambling

Nigeria’s AML regime treats casinos and gaming operators as high‑risk sectors, with industry reports highlighting weak underage verification and inconsistent enforcement of anti‑gambling laws as ongoing challenges; regulators and FSGRN reforms are explicitly aimed at tightening these gaps.

Operators are expected to implement robust know‑your‑customer (KYC) processes, including verification of identity, age and source of funds, and to use geo‑fencing and tracking to ensure bets are placed only within authorised jurisdictions and in accordance with licence conditions.

There is increasing emphasis on responsible gambling measures, including age verification, deposit and loss limits, self‑exclusion tools, and restrictions on advertising content and placement, with enforcement powers including fines and licence suspension for non‑compliant operators.

Key insight: Although enforcement is uneven, regulatory direction is clear – the compliance bar for AML, KYC and RG in Nigeria is rising, not falling.

11. Capital Requirement & Breakeven Considerations

Given the 11% GGR tax, ₦100 million per‑category licence fees and the need for serious marketing investment, a prudent two‑year capital plan for a Lagos‑anchored online sportsbook and casino would include:

  • Regulatory and licence costs in the low‑to‑mid seven‑figure USD range over two years (depending on NGN/USD rates and the number of categories and states targeted).
  • Infrastructure, platform and integration spend of several hundred thousand dollars to meet geo‑fencing, monitoring, AML/KYC and reporting requirements.
  • Marketing and working capital sufficient to absorb one to two years of negative EBITDA while the active player base is built and licence fees are amortised.

Key insight: Under the post‑2026 regime, Nigeria remains attractive in absolute revenue terms, but only operators with multi‑year horizons and sufficient capital to withstand early‑stage losses are likely to achieve sustainable profitability.
