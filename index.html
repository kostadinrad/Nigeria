Nigeria iGaming Strategic Report – March 2026

Following the November 2024 Supreme Court ruling that confined the National Lottery Act to the Federal Capital Territory, Nigeria has moved decisively to a state‑led model coordinated by the Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN). The country now hosts one of Africa’s largest gambling markets, supported by a young, mobile‑first population and recent fiscal reforms that standardise gaming taxes and licence fees across states.

Nigeria’s gambling industry is projected to generate about $3.63 billion by end‑2025, with over 60 million Nigerians placing bets and online gambling turnover estimated at ₦5.6 trillion, underscoring both scale and regulatory importance.

1. Market Landscape & Leading Brands

Multiple independent reports confirm that sports betting is the dominant form of gambling in Nigeria, accounting for the majority of the sector’s revenue, driven by widespread football fandom and mobile access. Industry commentary consistently cites local brands such as Bet9ja, BetKing and SportyBet among the leading operators.

  • Target demographic: Estimates suggest that over 60 million Nigerians place bets, with the core segment being 18–40‑year‑old males in urban and peri‑urban areas.
  • Device usage: The surge in online and mobile betting is tied to smartphone adoption and cheap data; digital and mobile channels now drive most betting transactions.
The Nigerian iGaming market is large and football‑centric, with tens of millions of regular bettors and a strong shift from retail to online and mobile platforms.

2. Regulatory Hierarchy & Licensing Paths

2.1 State Regulators & FSGRN

The Supreme Court decision clarified that lottery and related games fall under the residual powers of the states, affirming state control over gambling within their borders. In response, state regulators coordinated through the Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN), introducing a unified 11% tax on Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR) and fixed annual licence fees of ₦100 million per category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino), effective 1 January 2026.

The FSGRN regime applies equally to online and land‑based operators, requiring all licensees to comply with geo‑fencing and geo‑tracking obligations to respect state boundaries.

2.2 NLRC Federal Role & Remote Permits

The National Lottery Regulatory Commission (NLRC) retains its role within the FCT and continues to issue licences and remote permits, but these are now legally effective only in the FCT and are not sufficient for operations in the states without corresponding state licences.

For commercial planning, the FCT should be treated as a separate territory, while all other states are regulated via their own laws under the FSGRN‑coordinated framework.

4. Advertising Constraints & The Google Ban

Important Note: A major new constraint is the Google ban on gambling advertising targeting Nigeria, effective 8 January 2025, which prohibits the promotion of gambling products across Search, YouTube and the Display Network.

This ban forces operators to pivot to alternative channels such as affiliates, local influencers, SMS, in‑app cross‑promotion and offline sponsorships. Marketing efficiency has been structurally reduced, increasing Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) and requiring more expensive or less scalable acquisition channels.

7. Strategic Takeaways for Operators

  • Scale Requirements: Fixed ₦100m fees and the 11% GGR tax require high volume to reach break-even.
  • Marketing Pivot: The Google ad ban necessitates a heavy investment in influencers and affiliates.
  • Technical Integration: API-based monitoring and geo-fencing are mandatory for multi-state compliance.
