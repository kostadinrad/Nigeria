Nigeria iGaming Market Report – Sportsbook, Online Casino & Live Casino

Nigeria is a large, fast‑growing iGaming market (sportsbook, online casino and live casino) with a state‑led regulatory framework, an 11% tax on GGR from 2026, and high annual licence and operational fees for each licensed vertical.

1. Population & Connectivity

Nigeria has a population above 220 million, with a very young age structure and dense urban centres such as Lagos and Abuja that concentrate online gambling activity.

Industry research estimates that Nigeria’s online gambling market is growing at about 16% annually and is expected to reach around 500 million USD in revenue by the end of 2025, fuelled by high smartphone penetration, affordable data and widespread fintech adoption (wallets and local payment gateways).

Most online bets are placed via mobile devices, and operators rely heavily on instant electronic payment methods (bank transfers, cards, wallets) for deposits and withdrawals, making the ecosystem strongly mobile‑first.

Key insight: Infrastructure and payments are fully capable of supporting a mobile‑first sportsbook, RNG casino and live‑dealer ecosystem; disposable income per user, not connectivity, is the main constraint on ARPU.

2. Legalization Status

Before 2024, the National Lottery Act 2005 and its regulations formed the core federal framework for lotteries and some remote games, alongside state laws that regulated betting and gaming within individual states.

On 21 November 2024, the Supreme Court nullified the National Lottery Act 2005 as it applies to the 36 states, holding that the National Assembly lacks power to legislate on lotteries outside the Federal Capital Territory; the Act now applies only in the FCT, and states have exclusive authority over lotteries and games of chance in their territories.

Following the ruling, state regulators have asserted primary control over all gaming activities within their borders, and a Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN) has been formed to harmonise rules and tax structures across states, especially for online and remote products.

Key insight: Nigeria is now clearly a state‑led gambling market, with the National Lottery Act effectively confined to the FCT and state regulators taking centre stage for online and land‑based operations.

3. Regulatory Authority

The National Lottery Regulatory Commission (NLRC) continues to regulate lotteries and related games of chance within the FCT Abuja under the 2005 Act and still issues licences for national/remote lottery, sports betting and casino in that territory, but its licences no longer have legal force in the states after the Supreme Court decision.

States have their own regulators (for example, the Lagos State Lotteries and Gaming Authority) that license and supervise sports betting, lotteries, casinos and other games under state gaming laws; these bodies oversee licensing, technical standards, AML/KYC compliance and enforcement within their respective states.

The Federation of State Gaming Regulators of Nigeria (FSGRN) – a collective of state regulators – has introduced a harmonised tax and licensing regime that includes an 11% tax on GGR and a ₦100 million annual licence fee per major category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino) starting in 2026, aligning state practices while preserving decentralised control.

Key insight: Practically, operators must treat NLRC as FCT‑only and work primarily with state regulators and FSGRN when planning a commercial footprint across Nigeria.

4. Licence Classes & Product Scope

Under the combined FCT/state framework, the main licence classes relevant to online operators are:

  • Sports Betting Licences – covering pre‑match and live betting on domestic and international sports, including virtual sports, usually across retail outlets, websites and mobile apps.
  • Casino Licences – covering land‑based casinos and, in many states, online RNG and live‑dealer casino products (slots, tables, live roulette/blackjack/baccarat).
  • Lottery Licences – covering draw‑based lotteries, instant‑win games and, increasingly, mobile/SMS lotteries and promotional draws.
  • Other/Ancillary Licences – covering gaming machines, pool betting, “route operations” and other games that do not fit the standard categories.

A combined online operator (sportsbook + RNG casino + live casino) typically builds on:

  • A sports betting licence (or equivalent) for the sportsbook vertical; and
  • A casino licence (plus any specific remote/interactive authorisations) to operate RNG and live‑dealer casino products online.

Key insight: With a sports betting licence plus one or more casino/interactive licences across key states, a single licence set can cover a full online sportsbook, RNG casino and live‑dealer offering in Nigeria.

5. Mandatory Capital & Ownership

5.1 Minimum Capital & Fee Levels (Market Practice)

State regulators and FSGRN require operators to maintain substantial capital and pay high fixed fees:

  • Annual licence fee per major category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino): ₦100,000,000 (FSGRN standard from 2026 onwards).
  • Effective minimum capital requirements (via state rules and regulatory practice) are in the tens of millions of Naira, with additional proof of solvency, bank references and audited accounts required during licensing.

Although exact capital thresholds differ by state and are often stated in local currency, regulators generally require operators to show that they can sustain operations, honour player balances and invest in compliance and technology.

5.2 Local Company & Participation

Operators must establish a Nigerian company that holds the relevant licences and contracts; states may require directors or key management to be resident in Nigeria and favour structures with meaningful local operational control.

While there is no single statutory minimum local equity stake across all states, legal and regulatory commentary indicates that state regulators prefer (and sometimes insist on) some degree of local shareholding or joint venture structure in practice.

Key insight: High annual licence fees and local incorporation requirements function as a hard filter – only well‑capitalised operators with local structures can realistically enter and scale.

6. Physical Presence & Corporate Setup

Even for a purely online operation, regulators require a real presence in Nigeria in each licensing state: an administrative office, local staff, and identified key personnel (compliance, risk, IT, operations) who can be contacted and audited by the regulator.

Operators must register brand assets (name, logo, domain names) and integrate their platforms with state monitoring systems that provide real‑time or near‑real‑time visibility over stakes, payouts and player activity.

Key insight: “Pure offshore” remote operation without physical presence and integrated monitoring is not acceptable to Nigerian state regulators or FSGRN.

7. Licensing Process & Timeline

7.1 Main Steps

  1. Incorporation & Capitalisation
    Set up a Nigerian company, appoint directors, register for tax, and ensure sufficient capital and bank facilities to meet state and FSGRN requirements.
  2. Application Dossier to State Regulator(s)
    Submit application forms, corporate and shareholder documents, audited financials, and a 3–5 year business plan, including:
    • Business model and product mix (sportsbook, RNG casino, live casino).
    • Market and competitor analysis for Nigeria and specific states.
    • Financial projections and capital budgets.
    • Organisational chart and key personnel.
    • Platform and system description (sportsbook, casino, payments, risk management, limits).
  3. Due Diligence on Owners and Directors
    Provide police clearance, tax clearance, source‑of‑funds documentation and full KYC information for shareholders and directors.
  4. Technical and Compliance Review
    Demonstrate AML/KYC policies, transaction monitoring, geolocation controls, certified RNG and live‑dealer solutions, cybersecurity controls, logging, and regulator access to data and systems.
  5. Licence Issuance and Go‑Live
    After satisfying documentary, technical and financial requirements, pay the annual licence fee (e.g. ₦100m per category) and complete integration with regulatory monitoring; the operator may then commence marketing and onboarding players.

7.2 Timeline

Market and legal commentary suggest that realistic end‑to‑end timelines for a serious online operation – from incorporation through licensing and technical validation – range from 6 to 12 months, depending on the number of states and the complexity of the product mix.

Key insight: At least 6 months of “zero‑revenue” licensing and build‑out should be budgeted for Nigeria, with 9–12 months more realistic for a multi‑state, multi‑vertical operation.

8. Licence Fees & Regulatory OPEX

Under the FSGRN regime from 2026, key regulatory costs for each participating state include:

  • Annual licence fee per category (Lottery, Sports Betting, Casino): ₦100,000,000.
  • Application and processing charges, which are smaller but still material in aggregate.
  • Ongoing compliance costs for monitoring, audits, reporting and geo‑fencing requirements.

For a combined sportsbook + casino operator active in FCT, Lagos and one additional state, a realistic planning figure for total Year‑1 regulatory fees and compliance overhead is in the low‑to‑mid seven‑figure USD range, depending on the exchange rate and whether lottery licences are also obtained.

Note: In a live financial model, each licence fee and tax line item should be mapped directly from current FSGRN and state schedules; amounts can change as reforms are implemented.

9. Taxation (Official & Sectoral)

  • Gaming Tax: 11% of GGR (stakes minus winnings) from 1 January 2026 for all licensed operators in participating states, as announced by FSGRN and reported by multiple legal and industry sources.
  • Additional Levies: Some residual state or sector‑specific levies may apply until fully harmonised; operators should assume a blended 18–22% effective burden on GGR once tax and licence amortisation are combined.
  • Other Taxes: Standard corporate income tax and any excise or VAT‑type charges on telecommunications or digital services still apply according to general Nigerian tax law.

Key insight: The 11% GGR tax is now the central fiscal lever for gaming in Nigeria; financial models should treat this rate as a firm input for the medium term.

10. Market Landscape (Sportsbook, Casino, Live Casino)

Independent research describes Nigeria’s online gambling market as one of Africa’s fastest‑growing, with:

  • Sports betting dominating online volume, particularly on European football leagues and local competitions.
  • Rapid adoption of online slots, table games and live‑dealer content among urban, smartphone‑enabled users.

Dozens of international and domestic brands compete in the market, with major operators leveraging aggressive marketing, sponsorships and mobile‑optimised platforms to capture share in the 18–35 male demographic that drives most online betting activity.

Key insight: The market is competitive, mobile‑first and highly price‑sensitive; new entrants must budget substantial, sustained marketing and strong localisation.

11. Compliance, AML/KYC & Technical Requirements

Studies on gambling in Nigeria underline systemic risks around weak underage verification and historically limited enforcement; regulators and FSGRN reforms explicitly target these weaknesses by imposing stronger AML/KYC and technical requirements.

  • Customer identification and verification (ID, age, residence) is mandatory; operators must prevent under‑18 participation and use geo‑fencing to restrict play to licensed territories.
  • Operators must implement robust AML and counter‑terrorist financing programs: transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting, record keeping and periodic compliance audits.
  • Platforms must support secure infrastructure, event logging, and direct regulator access (APIs or dashboards) for oversight and investigations.

Responsible gambling obligations include self‑exclusion functions, deposit/loss limits, clear risk warnings, and compliance with advertising restrictions (content, timing, and audience targeting); regulators can sanction operators who breach these rules through fines, suspensions or licence revocation.

Key insight: Nigeria is moving rapidly toward a “tier‑1” compliance standard; any serious operator must budget for a high‑quality compliance and technology stack.
